Research on measurement and understanding of movement variability [17]
with nonlinear analyses has been well established in the last three [28]
decades in areas of biomechanics, sport science, psychology, cognitive science, [38]
and neuroscience \cite{davids2003}. [40]
Considering nonlinear analyses to quantify complexity of [47]
facial expressions variability, [50]
this work is hypothesising that such approach can be used to measure [62]
subtle variations of facial expressions in order to quantify [71]
different mental states (e.g. anxiety, disinterest, relief, etc) \cite{back2014}. [79]
Such hypothesis has then led the author to ask two research questions: [91]
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)] [91]
\item how the quantification of facial expressions can be related with [101]
the complexity of facial expressions?. [106]
\item does the quantification of the complexity of facial expressions [115]
variability can tell us something about the state of mind of a person?, [128]
\end{enumerate} [128]
[128]
Hence, with the investigation of nonlinear dynamics to [136]
quantify movement variability in human-humanoid interaction [142]
\cite{XochicalePhDThesis2018}, this work is proposing [146]
the use of Recurrence Quantification Analysis (RQA) [153]
to quantify the complexity of facial expressions to give [162]
insights into the raised questions. [167]
RQA computes measurements based on the density of [175]
recurrence points of diagonal or vertical line [182]
structures in Recurrence Plots \cite{marwan2007} [186]
to provide understanding of the dynamics of a system [195]
i.e. the determinism (predictability) or Shannon entropy (complexity). [203]
This work presents a pilot experiment of one participant (the author) [214]
who were asked to perform three levels of [222]
face expressions: [224]
(i) neutral variation, (ii) slow variation and (iii) faster variations [234]
to show the variability of facial expressions. [241]
Then, using 67 2D face landmarks time series collected [250]
with OpenFace \cite{baltrusaitis2018}, this work shows 3D [256]
RQA Entr plots that can be used to quantify complexity of [267]
face expressions and therefore relate such metric [274]
to subtle variations of facial expressions [280]
related to the state of mind of the participant. [289]
